<p class="dropcap">
  AVE-RD is a pilot public-policy project that evaluates the effect of a
  video-based information campaign on the schooling decisions of Dominican
  students in grades 7&ndash;12. Implemented by the Ministry of Education,
  evaluated by IDEICE and J-PAL LAC, funded by USAID and MINERD with
  follow-up support from INICIA Educaci&oacute;n. The study is a direct
  scale-up of <a href="https://academic.oup.com/qje/article-abstract/125/2/515/1882172">Robert Jensen&rsquo;s 2010
  <em>Quarterly Journal of Economics</em> paper</a> on perceived returns
  to education in the Dominican Republic.
</p>

<div class="section-header reveal">
  <div class="eyebrow eyebrow--rule">Origins</div>
  <h2>From Jensen 2010 to a national policy</h2>
  <p class="lede">
    AVE did not start as a clean-sheet design. It started as the answer
    to a specific puzzle Robert Jensen documented in
    <span class="font-osf">2010</span>: Dominican eighth-graders
    underestimated the wage returns to secondary schooling, and a small
    information intervention closed part of that gap.
  </p>
</div>

<p>
  Jensen surveyed eighth-grade boys in <span class="font-osf">150</span>
  clusters across the Dominican Republic and asked them to estimate
  earnings at different schooling levels. Perceived returns were far
  below measured returns. He then randomly assigned schools to receive
  a short, factual presentation of the actual earnings-by-schooling-level
  data. The intervention raised completed schooling by
  <span class="font-osf">0.20&ndash;0.35</span> additional years over
  the four years that followed &mdash; a striking effect for a
  one-shot information delivery, and the empirical anchor for the
  paper that became a foundational citation for the
  &ldquo;information matters&rdquo; literature in education.
</p>

<p>
  AVE-RD is the operational scale-up of that finding. Same country,
  same theoretical premise, but four moves further:
</p>

<ol class="numbered-list reveal">
  <li>
    <div>
      <h3>Same gap, different delivery</h3>
      <p>
        Jensen used a research-team presentation in a sub-sample of
        schools. AVE-RD encodes the same logic into a four-episode
        video series that a counselor or teacher can run in any
        classroom &mdash; making the intervention deliverable through
        the existing MINERD orientation network rather than through
        researcher field staff.
      </p>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li>
    <div>
      <h3>Two arms, not one</h3>
      <p>
        AVE-RD splits the intervention into a <em>persuasive</em> arm
        (qualitative framing through eighth-grade characters) and an
        <em>informative</em> arm (the persuasive arm plus quantitative
        wage statistics). That separation lets us test whether
        information alone is doing the work, or whether the persuasive
        framing carries effect on its own.
      </p>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li>
    <div>
      <h3>Two waves, not one</h3>
      <p>
        Schools in the first batch returned for a second screening a
        year later. The twice-treated cohort lets us look at dosing &mdash;
        and it is in that subgroup that the test-score effects are
        largest.
      </p>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li>
    <div>
      <h3>National scale, not a sub-sample</h3>
      <p>
        Jensen&rsquo;s sample was a research-grade sub-sample. The
        AVE-RD pilot reached <span class="font-osf">600</span> public
        schools and <span class="font-osf">43,000</span> students at
        baseline; the post-evaluation policy adoption brought the
        intervention to <em>every public school in the country</em>.
      </p>
    </div>
  </li>
</ol>

<div class="signal-panel reveal">
  <div class="eyebrow">Sister projects · Per&uacute;, Chile, Colombia</div>
  <p>
    The same design has been implemented in three other countries.
    <strong>DFM Per&uacute;</strong> &mdash; same telenovela video,
    funded by the US Department of Labor, managed by IPA and J-PAL with
    MINEDU's MineduLAB &mdash; reduced school dropout by
    <span class="font-osf">18.8</span>% in urban areas at
    <span class="font-osf">$0.05</span> per student and is now
    standing policy across all <span class="font-osf">2,001</span>
    Peruvian secondary schools with full class days. The two younger
    siblings &mdash; <strong>DFM Chile</strong> (J-PAL Santiago, UC,
    Mineduc Chile) and <strong>DFM Colombia / ICFES-Bot</strong>
    (ConsiliumBots with ICFES, reaching ~430,000 high-school seniors a
    year on WhatsApp) &mdash; have moved from televised video to
    digital platform and chatbot, demonstrating that the design
    travels across delivery surfaces too. Two countries at national
    policy, four implementations, three research institutions. See
    <a href="/projects/sister-projects/">Sister projects</a> for the
    full comparison and the ConsiliumBots origin story.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="section-header reveal">
  <div class="eyebrow eyebrow--rule">The funding context</div>
  <h2>Why USAID, why MINERD, why now INICIA</h2>
</div>

<p>
  USAID&rsquo;s Dominican Republic mission funded the AVE-RD pilot
  (<span class="font-osf">2014&ndash;2017</span>) under a cooperative
  agreement with J-PAL LAC and MINERD. The agreement was structured
  around <strong>thirteen milestones</strong> covering instrument
  design, school-level randomization, two waves of fieldwork, the
  Pruebas Nacionales linkage, and a final endline report. The
  <a href="/projects/download-videos-2/">endline report at
  Milestone <span class="font-osf">13</span></a> documented the
  dropout, test-score, and belief-update effects that subsequently
  drove the policy-adoption decision at MINERD.
</p>

<p>
  MINERD funded the in-school operation out of the Department of
  Orientation and Psychology base budget &mdash; the partnership was
  set up so that field delivery used <em>existing</em> ministry
  capacity rather than parallel research-team field staff. That
  decision is part of why the program could survive the end of the
  USAID grant and become standing ministry policy.
</p>

<p>
  For the <span class="font-osf">2025&ndash;2027</span> long-run
  follow-up, the funding architecture is updated. MINERD continues to
  carry field operations. IDEICE is the data steward and brokers the
  Tesorer&iacute;a de la Seguridad Social earnings linkage.
  <a href="https://www.iniciaeducacion.org/">INICIA Educaci&oacute;n</a>
  &mdash; the Vicini family&rsquo;s Dominican education foundation
  &mdash; is the lead domestic funder. The
  <a href="/projects/follow-up/">follow-up plan</a> describes the
  remaining funding gap, primarily concentrated in the cohort
  re-survey and the open replication kit.
</p>

<div class="section-header reveal">
  <div class="eyebrow eyebrow--rule">The hypothesis</div>
  <h2>Information gaps and schooling decisions</h2>
  <p class="lede">
    Students choose how long to stay in school. Their choice depends on what they
    believe the returns are. Where those beliefs are wrong, an information
    campaign should move behavior. AVE-RD tested that hypothesis.
  </p>
</div>

<p>
  Two video variants &mdash; an <em>informative</em> arm with statistics on
  income returns to schooling, and a <em>persuasive</em> arm structured as a
  short TV series &mdash; were rolled out in two batches: May <span class="font-osf">2015</span>
  and April <span class="font-osf">2016</span>. Schools that participated in
  the first batch returned for the second.
</p>

<p>
  Preliminary results indicate that students tend to undervalue the benefit of
  more education, while overvaluing the benefit of leaving school early. The
  campaign appears to close that gap where it exists.
</p>

<div class="signal-panel signal-panel--research reveal">
  <div class="eyebrow">Beyond the pilot</div>
  <p>
    Following the evaluation, MINERD adopted AVE as standing policy. The
    four videos and the classroom protocol are now delivered in
    <strong>every public school in the Dominican Republic</strong>. The
    <span class="font-osf">2025&ndash;2027</span> follow-up wave operates
    on top of that policy backdrop &mdash; the original cohort is now
    <span class="font-osf">23&ndash;26</span> years old and reachable
    through MINERD enrolment records and the Tesorer&iacute;a de la
    Seguridad Social earnings panel.
  </p>
</div>

<div class="section-header reveal">
  <div class="eyebrow eyebrow--rule">Findings</div>
  <h2>Four effects across the treated cohort</h2>
</div>

<ol class="numbered-list reveal">
  <li>
    <div>
      <h3>Less willingness to leave school early</h3>
      <p>Treated students reported lower intent to drop out, higher likelihood of secondary graduation, higher probability of a college degree.</p>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li>
    <div>
      <h3>Reduced dropout rate</h3>
      <p>Statistically significant reductions across grades 7&ndash;12.</p>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li>
    <div>
      <h3>Higher National Test scores</h3>
      <p>Treated students scored higher on the Pruebas Nacionales than untreated peers in the same school.</p>
    </div>
  </li>
  <li>
    <div>
      <h3>Channels beyond information alone</h3>
      <p>Effect sizes exceeded the pure-information model&rsquo;s prediction, suggesting identity, aspiration and peer-perception channels.</p>
    </div>
  </li>
</ol>
